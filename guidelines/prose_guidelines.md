# Prose guidelines

Third person, close POV, past tense, external only.

## 1. Evidence prose — the camera test

Narration may contain only what a camera and microphone placed in the
scene would record. The narrator is a transparent witness, not a voice.

Before output, test every narration sentence: could a camera have
captured this? If it contains interpretation, it fails.

Banned moves in narration:
- Mind-reading: "suggested", "seemed", "as though he thought",
  "decided", "didn't care" — any claim about what a character thinks,
  feels, or intends.
- Narrator judgment: "should have", "deserved", "unreasonable" — any
  evaluation of an action.
- Personification of objects: swords are not "patient" or
  "unimpressed"; blades do not "show no interest". The blade didn't
  move. Say that.
- Speculation lists: "didn't notice, or didn't care, or had decided..."
  The camera records one reality, not alternatives.
- Interpretive framing: "the kind of X that Y" constructions.
- Metaphor and simile in narration. (Characters may use them in
  dialogue — that is them speaking, not the narrator.)

Non-audiovisual senses (smell, taste, touch, temperature) cannot reach
the camera directly. They enter the scene only through a character's
visible reaction or spoken words.

Interior states are never narrated. They are externalized: shown
through action, posture, pace, or said out loud by a character.

Violation -> fix (from real output):
- "The corridor was the kind of quiet that had something hiding in it."
  -> "The corridor was quiet. Somewhere ahead, metal scraped."
- "...at an angle that suggested it had landed there by accident and
  he had decided to keep it."
  -> "The crown sat askew over one ear."
- "The blade showed no interest in coming free."
  -> "The blade didn't move."
- "He pulled with the kind of focus that deserved a better result."
  -> "He set his feet wider and pulled. Nothing."

Canonical example of externalizing (interior -> evidence):
- WRONG (processing): "The cat headed down the corridor toward the
  kitchens."
- RIGHT (evidence): "The cat walked along the tight, dim service
  corridor. He stopped, sniffed the air. 'This way. Definitely.'"
  The destination is never named by the narrator; the cat's behavior
  and words carry it.

## 2. Flashlight — relevance

Every detail must earn its place. A detail earns its place by doing at
least one of: setting up a payoff, paying off a setup, characterizing
through action, or staging space the action is about to use. A detail
that does none of these is cut, however pretty.

The flashlight points at what matters for THIS beat (the directing
notes say what that is). Everything outside the flashlight stays dark —
no inventory of the room, no atmosphere for its own sake.

## 3. The wave — AAA structure (anticipation, action, absorption)

Every significant moment gets three phases, each with enough words
(camera time) to work:

- Anticipation: build the question before the answer. The sound before
  its source. The pause before the pull.
- Action: deliver cleanly and briefly. The action itself is short —
  do not decorate the strike.
- Absorption: hold after the action while the reader processes.
  Reactions, stillness, silence. Do not cut away early; do not fill
  the silence with narration.

Failure modes: rushing absorption (cutting to the next thing
immediately), padding action (slow-motion decoration), skipping
anticipation (answer arrives before the question exists).

## 4. Staging through action

Space and objects enter the scene when a character touches, uses,
crosses, or reacts to them — never as standalone description. The
room is revealed by moving through it. If nobody interacts with it in
this beat and it stages nothing, it does not exist.

## 5. Layers — text, context, subtext

- Text: what is said and done. This is all the narrator gives.
- Context: what the arrangement of the scene means. Built by staging,
  never stated.
- Subtext: what the characters avoid saying. Built by what they say
  instead, never explained.

The narrator never surfaces context or subtext. If the layering
worked, the reader assembles it; if the narrator states it, the layer
is destroyed.

## 6. Experience, not information — the riddle

Every scene is a riddle the reader parses on their own. That parsing
is the engagement.

- Effect before cause. Sound before source. Reaction before reveal.
- Never pre-label a reveal ("it was the fox trying to steal the
  sword" before we see him).
- Never explain what a beat meant after it lands.
- Trust the reader: if the evidence is staged right, the conclusion
  is theirs to make — and they will make it.

## 7. Full resolution — evidence prose is still prose

The bans in rule 1 forbid interpretation. They do not forbid prose.
Documentary is not telegraphic.

Observable facts have unlimited resolution. The camera can zoom: how a
paw lands, how weight shifts through a pull, what a sound does as
distance closes, how light moves when a door swings, what the grip
does between one attempt and the next. Spending words on observable
texture is the writer's job. A rushed moment is as much a failure as
an interpreted one.

The scene is continuous footage. Its internal structure (movements,
phases) is scaffolding and must be invisible in the output: no
separators, no section breaks, no headers. Bridge from moment to
moment through motion, sound, and physical continuity in the scene
itself. A hard cut is allowed only when the location changes.

Sentence rhythm carries the wave:
- anticipation runs in longer, connected sentences that carry the
  reader forward;
- action snaps short;
- absorption settles into medium, even sentences that hold.
Uniform staccato is a defect equal to purple prose. Short sentences
are an impact tool; used everywhere, they hit nothing.
